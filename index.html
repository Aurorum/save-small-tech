Issue

Issue

Based on government promotion of the Online Safety Bill, you might think it is designed to target social media giants: so-called “Big Tech”. That is not the case. The government’s Impact Assessment estimates that 25K services would be liable to the new regulations. This is a vast underestimate, and either misguided or misleading. It arbitrarily assumes that 3K “small platforms” would fall under the Bill, such as forums, when that aspect would certainly involve tens of thousands of platforms alone. The government’s working assumption is utterly wrong. With limited exemptions for some comment sections and reviews, all online services are in-scope. This means individuals, hobbyists and businesses would have mountains of incomprehensible red tape imposed on them.

The government has stated that Ofcom will produce “easy to understand guidance” to avoid individuals needing to seek legal advice. Ofcom has also acknowledged that the “largest online services have capabilities and resources that vastly outstrip those of most inscope services”, and have promised proportionality in their response. It is inevitable that most smaller services will not fulfil these new legal duties, and likely that Ofcom will not take enforcement against them all. But this current arrangement would involve decent and ordinary citizens placing their head in the tiger of Ofcom, and hoping it doesn’t bite. There would be chilling ramifications across the Internet when a smaller platform - a hobby project of an individual - inevitably becomes targeted. Large swathes of the Internet would be considered criminal. With the Secretary of State setting directions over Ofcom, it risks even becoming a politicised affair.

The principle of the Internet has been about freedom, accessibility, and openness: that at no cost, anyone with an idea can contribute an innovation about what matters to them. This Bill ends that. Launching a digital project no longer becomes about mere technical ability. The regulations will disproportionately deter individuals from creating services online, who have neither the money or resources to comply with them, infringing their civil liberties on freedom of speech. Pragmatically, many overseas services who lack the resources to comply will simply block British access, as over one thousand American sites did following the introduction of GDPR. This Bill proposes restrictions far more onerous than that, and the consequence will be British residents losing access to the services which they enjoy most.

Without these changes, the position of incumbents will be consolidated. At the time of politicians promoting “cutting red tape”, they would destroy small businesses; while raising hopes for “unicorn startups”, they would ensure the next great tech project could almost certainly never be British; and amid claims of “levelling up”, the Internet - perhaps the greatest vehicle of opportunity - would become inaccessible and closed.

Solutions

Solutions

Return thresholds

Previous ministers floated the idea of thresholds as to when the Bill takes effect by default, which would remove the regulatory burden from individuals and businesses who lack the resources to deal with them. This could take the form of a size threshold, which excludes services that have less than a certain number of employees. Alternatively, a worldwide turnover threshold could be used, especially since liabilities are financial. Services with more resources have more scope for causing harm.

Repeal Section 177(2)

Section 177(2) gives the Secretary of State power to withdraw exemptions, even without consulting Ofcom. At the stroke of a pen, the government would have the power to jeopardise swathes of the Internet: every single blog, and every single online store. It undermines the stated aim of a “predictable framework”, and massively threatens excessive state control. This decision should be made by the people’s representatives, and left solely for Parliament to amend in primary legislation.

Require accountability

This approach should be clearly defined, with the thresholds written in primary legislation rather than left to Ofcom or the Secretary of State. It would be reasonable for the Bill to grant either of them powers enabling these thresholds to be overridden for specific services. These must be reserved for the very rare occasions where genuine harm has already been caused by a service, and should be subject to either parliamentary or judicial oversight.

Rework Section 189(2)

Section 189 uses “catch-all clauses” to ensure that the majority of online services are in-scope through the backdoor, even though the government claim otherwise. The listed functionalities of what makes an in-scope service should be substantially more specific. 189(2)(m) defining scope through the use of hyperlinks should be removed entirely. It is normal practice for almost all websites to do this, and a key principle of the Internet.

Other Problems

Other Problems

The focus of this campaign is around the scope of the Bill, which has not been debated properly. The government’s Impact Assessment has too easily been accepted as fact by Parliament, even though it does not add up. Changing the scope of the Bill would be a massive success. It would save small services, keep the Internet more accessible to innovation, and prevent a monopoly of “Big Tech” - all without compromising online safety.

But there are many other areas of this Bill which are flawed, most of which have been taken up by other civil liberty organisations:

  • The government will have unprecedented powers to determine what is “legal but harmful”, and consequently what should be censored online
  • Social media companies will be encouraged to “err on the side of censorship” at the threat of huge fines and criminal liability, using automated technology that fails to understand the context or intent of posts
  • Children - including teenagers, and those above the age of consent - risk losing access to valuable online resources that have become part of their routine (eg. YouTube) in the name of “preventing harm” when parental restrictions already exist on a broadband level
  • Some services (including ones not focused on adult content) will be mandated to collect IDs, making the Internet more inaccessible and exacerbating the risk of database breaches - there were multiple suicides when this happened over a dating site encouraging affairs in 2015
  • Encryption will be undermined by the Bill targeting private messages, threatening digital privacy and those who rely on it for their own safety
  • Journalists are granted additional freedom of speech protections - but why should the state determine who is a journalist, and why can’t all users receive them?
  • Thresholds for the new “false communication” and “harmful communication” offences, which carry up to two-year custodial sentences, are low and based on perception of another individual rather than the defendant’s intent
  • Ofcom may withhold details of enforcement “not appropriate for publication”, including when they have sought a court order (“Access Restriction Order”) to remove an entire service from British access. It is unclear if this will be subject to FOI disclosure either
